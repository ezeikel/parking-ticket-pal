Adjudicators decisions for Moving Traffic Offences

On this page we have summerised over 50 adjudicator’s decisions which apply to different types of moving traffic contravention. For example yellow boxes, banned motor vehicles, not complying with an arrow, no right turn etc etc. Simply scroll down to the heading that applies to you and you will see cases where motorists have won – all summerised with case numbers.

Penalty charge notice not received by the motorist and defects in a penalty charge notice

In the case of Bell Vs London Borough of Southwark, a penalty charge notice had been issued by post for a parking contravention. The appellant stated that he had not received the PCN even though it had been posted by the council. Although the adjudicator said that where legislation provides for the service of a document by post service was deemed to have been effected 2 working days after the document was posted, he said this presumption was rebuttable. The local authority proceeded to issue a notice to owner because they had not received payment for the penalty, but the adjudicator found that they were not entitled to serve a notice to owner because the penalty charge notice had not been properly ‘issued’ under the 1991 act. He allowed the motorists appeal on the basis that the motorist had not received the penalty charge notice posted by the council.

 

CASE NO: 207 0117279 – Transport for London
Battersea Bridge Road/ Westbridge Road

This is an interesting case where a vehicle was hired but that the person who received the Penalty Charge Notice said that it was not his signature on the hire agreement.  The adjudicator said that “the position in law is that if the hire agreement is not signed, the Appellant is not deemed to be the owner.  On the evidence as it stands, I am not satisfied that the signature on the hiring agreement is the Appellant’s and I am therefore not satisfied that he is liable as the owner of the vehicle”.  The Appeal was allowed.  If you have not hired a vehicle or signed the hire agreement, then liability for a Penalty Charge Notice cannot be passed to you.

CASE NO: 207 0107195 – Borough of Islington
Morris Place

This is an interesting case as the Appellant said he did not receive the initial Penalty Charge Notice because it may either have been intercepted or because there were so many flats in his block and the postman delivered to a central point.  The adjudicator said “that the crucial Penalty Charge Notice was not served effectively that is to say ‘actually delivered’”.   The Appeal was allowed.  The local authority has to prove that the Penalty Charge Notice is correctly served.  Simply posting it to a motorist is not proof that the motorist actually received it – an interesting point!

CASE NO: 206 0292035 – Transport for London
Battersea Bridge

The position on this is that the owner of the vehicle said that he did not receive the original Penalty Charge Notice.   Transport for London could only show that the PCN was printed.  But, in any event, the adjudicator accepted that the Appellant did not receive the Penalty Charge Notice and therefore the Appeal was allowed.   It is up to the local authority to ensure that a Penalty Charge Notice is received.

Enforcement by CCTV

 

206 – 0543532 – London Borough of Islington
Leonard Street

This case was won because the Notice of Rejection issued by the Local Authority was not compliant with Schedule 6, paragraph 4 of the Road Traffic Act 1991 which states as follows:-

“Where any representations are made under paragraph 2 above but the Local Authority concerned do not accept that a ground has been established, the Notice served under paragraph 2(7) above (the Notice of Rejection) must:

a)    state that a Charge Certificate may be served under Paragraph 6 below unless before the end of the period of 28 days beginning with the date of service of the Notice of Rejection:

(i)    the Penalty Charge Notice is paid; or
(ii)    the person on whom the Notice is served appeals to a parking adjudicator against a Penalty Charge;

b)    Indicate the nature of a parking adjudicator’s power to award costs against any person appealing to him; and

c)    Describe in general terms the form and manner in which an Appeal to a parking adjudicator must be made and may contain such other information as the Authority consider appropriate.

However, the Notice of Rejection issued by Islington in this instance stated: Therefore within 28 days of the date of this letter, you should either pay the Penalty Charge or Appeal against our decision and if you do not do either – pay or appeal within the 28 day period, we may issue a Charge Certificate.”

It was argued that the wording does not make it clear that the period commences from the date of service of the letter and not the date of the letter itself and that therefore the Notice of Rejection is not compliant.  The Appellant supported his case by reference to another case at MPAS which was Case No: AY05003B (which was Lukha v Aylesbury District Council).

The adjudicator found in favour of the Appellant and stated “I am satisfied that the above authorities indicate that a strict interpretation is required when considering the mandatory requirements of the statute.  The Notice of Rejection in this case does not comply with those requirements and is therefore invalid and accordingly I must allow the Appeal”.  There are very strict guidelines on not just the wording of a Penalty Charge Notice but also other Notices that may be served during the process such as, in this case, the Notice of Rejection.  It is important to note that the 28 day period section on a Notice of Rejection must state as follows “The enforcing authority may disregard any such representations which are received by them after the period of 28 days beginning with the date on which the Penalty Charge Notice in question was served”.

CASE NO: 206 0542755 – City of Westminster
Oxford Street

In this case, the Penalty Charge Notice showed 6th November 2006 and the date of issue of 6th November 2006, however, the audit trail produced by Westminster clearly showed the adjudicator that the Penalty Charge Notice could not have been issued for at least two days after the 6th November.  The adjudicator allowed the Appeal and said “it is not open to the City of Westminster to produce false copies in evidence!”.

CASE NO: 206 0164512 – London Borough of Islington
Theberton Street

In this case there was no date of issue on the Notice, only the date of contravention.  Therefore the Appeal was allowed.  A Penalty Charge Notice must state both the date of issue and the date that the contravention actually occurred for it to be lawful even if the 2 dates are the same.

CASE NO: 206 0176963 – London Borough of Islington
Theberton Street

As was the case before, the Penalty Charge Notice did not state the date of the Notice “it is therefore invalid and unenforceable and I must accordingly allow this Appeal”. A Penalty Charge Notice must state both the date of issue and the date that the contravention actually occurred for it to be lawful even if the dates are the same.

CASE NO: 206 0109514 – City of Westminster
Oxford Street

This is another case where Westminster’s Penalty Charge Notice that did not show the date of the Notice.  The adjudicator said “the PCN issued to the Appellant’s vehicle records the date of the alleged contravention but does not include the “date of the Notice”.  The PCN does not therefore comply with Section 66.  It is consequently invalid and the Appellant is not obliged to pay the Penalty Charge”.  The Appeal was allowed.  A Penalty Charge Notice must state both the date of issue and the date that the contravention actually occurred for it to be lawful.

CASE NO: 207 0143971 – Waltham Forest Council
Hoe Street E17

This case concerned a Penalty Charge Notice that was dated over 28 days after the alleged contravention.  Also, no unit of currency was specified for the full penalty charge.  The adjudicator said that “either A or B would be fatal”.  Therefore, the adjudicator did not need to adjudicate.

Quite unbelievable that an authority would try and obtain money in this way.

CASE NO: 206 0096740 – Camden Council
Laystall Street

This was an interesting one as the Council rejected the Applicant’s formal Appeal but on the Notice of Rejection it referred to the Applicant’s Right of Appeal “28 days from the date hereof” whereas the Act requires the reference to say “28 days from the date of service” – which must, ‘inevitably’ be after 28 days from the date of issue.   This is the second Appeal to be upheld on this basis, the first was by the National Parking Adjudication Service in the case of Lukha v Aylesbury Vale District Council.  The Appeal was allowed.  Not only must the wording on the Penalty Charge Notice comply but also the text on the Notice of Rejection must comply.

CASE NO: 207 000963A – London Borough of Croydon
North End Croydon

The adjudicator was satisfied that the contravention occurred but that the owner had sold his car and produced the letter from the DVLA confirming this.   The Appeal was allowed.  You are not liable for a Penalty Charge Notice in respect of a vehicle that you did not own at the time.

Adjudicator’s decisions relating to a yellow box junction enforcement (Contravention Code 31)

In the following cases the motorists appeals were all allowed. You can refer the council or the independent adjudicator to these cases if you received a penalty charge notice in similar circumstances. Each case is of course considered on its own merits.

Entering and stopping in a box-junction when prohibited

Case number 2100203420 LB Hammersmith and Fulham

In this case the motorist did enter the box when the exit was not clear but the box was not marked correctly. It has double yellow lines running along the kerb. The original plan on the approved authorisation for enforcement issued by the department of transport did not have the double yellow lines. The appeal was allowed.

There should not be double yellow lines within a yellow box.

Place Invaders Ltd vs. Transport for London (Published Case)

In this case the parking adjudicator upheld the appeal of Place Invaders because “the evidence produced by Transport for London in support of its case  does not, however, show the entry of the vehicle into the box; it commences later, when the vehicle is already stopped in the box”.  It seems to me that in order properly to consider whether the contravention has occurred the video recording needs to show the entry of the vehicle into the box since that is the start of the event that will or will not lead to the there being a contravention.  In the absence of this evidence, bearing in mind that the burden is on TfL to prove the contravention I cannot find that it has discharged its burden of proof and I accordingly allow this appeal”.

CASE NO: 206 0183437 – London Borough of Camden
High Holborn

In this case, the taxi driver’s Appeal was upheld because of “the fact that at the time that he caused his vehicle to enter the box junction he did not have to stop within the box junction due to the presence of stationary vehicles, he could have carried on even if a bit off the line he wanted to follow”.

The offence is entering a box-junction when your exit is blocked by stationary traffic.  Although this motorist stopped in the box junction, at the time that he entered it an exit lane was clear even if it wasn’t the lane in front of him so his Appeal was allowed.

Case No: 2100226302 London Borough of Enfield

This was a very simple case. The video evidence simply didn’t show the vehicle entering the box when the exit was not clear. Appeal allowed!

It is not a contravention to stop in a box. Only to enter it when the exit is blocked by other vehicles unless you are turning right.

CASE NO: 207 0120149 – Transport For London
Maida Vale/Clarendon Terrace

In this case the vehicle stopped within the box junction but Transport for London were unable to “show the Appellant’s vehicle at the moment it actually entered the box junction”.  “I cannot find as a fact that on this particular occasion a contravention did occur.  Accordingly, this Appeal must be allowed”.

Once again, the offence is that of entering a box junction when the exit is obscured by stationary traffic and not that of actually stopping in a box junction.

CASE NO: 207 0077276 – London Borough of Ealing
South Road

In this case the video evidence showed the Appellant’s vehicle entering and stopping in the box junction but it did not actually show the exit of the box junction being blocked by stationary traffic at the time that the vehicle entered the box junction. Therefore, the adjudicator was not satisfied that the contravention actually occurred and allowed the Appeal.

CASE NO: 207 0170056 – Borough of Lambeth
Norwood Road

This was one where a CCTV camera mounted in another vehicle was used to enforce a yellow box.  The adjudicator said “it may be difficult for a local authority ever to be able to prove this particular type of contravention on such evidence”.  The vehicle with the CCTV camera in was parked just beyond the box and therefore the camera could not show the exit to the box being blocked by stationary vehicles at the time that the motorists entered it. As the offence is that of entering the box when the exit is obscured, there was no way that the local authority on the video evidence submitted could show this and the case was therefore allowed.

CASE NO: 207 0143960 – London Borough of Ealing
Cherington Road, W7

This is an important case as it is in a situation which is quite common in London.   The Appellant said that he had to stop within the box junction to allow a pedestrian to cross the road.  The evidence did show that the Appellant’s vehicle entered the box junction when her exit was not clear and this is of course an offence.  However, the offence only occurs if “an exit to the junction is obstructed by another vehicle”.   The adjudicator said “the video evidence showed that [the Appellant] fails to clear the junction only because the traffic lights change and pedestrians started to cross.  In the circumstances, the contravention relied on by the local authority did not occur”.  The Appeal was allowed.  There are many situations in London where the exit to a yellow box can be obscured by traffic lights turning to red.  If this is the case and the traffic lights are red, even if they are red at the time that you enter the box junction, no offence has been committed and an offence has only been committed if your exit is obscured by another vehicle (not pedestrians crossing, a set of traffic lights).

CASE NO mv007ne02 – London borough of Newham

Barking Road junc Wellington Road

The council provided stills of the appellant’s vehicle entering the box when the traffic ahead was blocking the right hand Lane. There were 2 straight ahead lanes and the appellant’s vehicle was in the right hand lane. It stopped in the yellow box due to the stationary vehicles ahead. However the left hand lane was not blocked and traffic was still moving. The adjudicator said that the appellant could have moved into the left hand lane in which case he would have cleared the box but he chose not to. This was his choice. The adjudicator said “He chose to stay in the right hand lane and it was his choice  to do that which prevented him from not clearing the junction ahead rather than the presence of stationary vehicles.” The appeal was allowed.

Remember the contravention is entering a box junction when the exit is blocked by stationary vehicles. It is not a contravention to stop in a box junction.

CASE NO: 207 0117202 – Transport for London
East India Dock Road/Crisp St

The Appeal was upheld because “I find as fact that the criss-cross yellow box markings are faded and unclear.  I find that the authority has not complied with their obligations to provide clear and adequate signage.  It follows that I allow the Appeal”.

If the yellow box junction is faded then it is unenforceable.

CASE NO:  207 0008739 – London Borough of Camden
Fitzjohns Avenue

The Appeal was allowed because “Firstly, that the Appellant’s vehicle had encroached onto the box-junction very slightly, secondly, that there was a reasonable expectation that the Appellant would have travelled through the box-junction and finally that if the other vehicle seen in the CCTV footage had not turned left in front of the Appellant’s vehicle, it would not have entered the box junction”.

Here you can see that local authorities will try and penalise a motorist who is only very slightly encroached on a yellow box and at a time when entering a box, the motorist expected to be able to clear it on the other side.

CASE NO: 206 026837A – London Borough of Camden
High Holborn

The Appellant argued that his vehicle was at no time stationary and as the Council provided still images, the adjudicator was not able to determine whether it was in fact stationary or not.  The adjudicator said that “on balance, I am not satisfied that the case can be proved to the standard required”.

CASE NO: 207 0136364 – Transport for London
Bows Road/Green Lanes

This is an interesting case where adjudicator looked at the layout diagrams set out by the Department of Transport.  Transport for London stated that the two yellow box junctions were actually whole junction boxes and each was at a junction of two roads.  The adjudicator, however, said that they were basically on the Westbound and Eastbound carriageways of the same road, being the North Circular Road, even though it was a dual carriageway.  On this basis, the yellow box junction should have extended into all four corners of a four-way junction but in effect they had two yellow boxes next to each other on the same road with a gap between them. The adjudicator said “I was therefore not satisfied that the area where each of these box junction markings is laid out is a junction between two or more roads.  Consequently the box junction marking on the Westbound carriageway of Bowes Road on which TFL rely in order to pursue this penalty charge does not comply with either of the diagrams in the TRSGD.  Accordingly, TFL may not enforce this penalty so I allow the Appeal”.

This is very interesting as many box junctions are not laid out correctly and therefore are unenforceable. Quite often in London we see 2 boxes next to each other on multi lane roads. See picture above of Marylebone Road junction with Gloucester Place.

Transport for London continued to enforce this box even after the adjudicator rules it unenforceable.!

CASE NO: 207 0119729 – London Borough of Haringey
Green Lanes N4

This case was upheld for two reasons.

Firstly, the Penalty Charge Notice was not specific as to the location of the box and as Green Lane was a very long road the Penalty Charge Notice was not compliant as it did not specify the exact location.

Secondly, however, all four corners of the road markings of the yellow box must meet a kerb-line.  What has happened here is that the yellow box not only covered the four-way junction but it extended further along the road towards a further set of traffic lights.  It was therefore an irregularly shaped box which did not meet the kerb in all four corners and had it not extended along one carriageway, towards the set of lights, the motorist would not have committed an offence.  The Appeal was therefore upheld.  Please see the diagrams of the yellow box because quite often in London they are not set out in accordance with the regulations and are therefore unenforceable.   In fact it is illegal for a local authority to enforce a box which they know does not comply with the Department of Transport guidelines.

CASE NO: 207 0113584 – London Borough of Camden
Fitzjohns Avenue NW3

Here the motorist contended that his vehicle actually stalled.  Although he committed an offence of entering a yellow box junction when the exit was not clear, there was nothing in the video evidence that was incompatible with a car having stalled.  This is an interesting one as how would a Council be able to prove that an engine had not stalled and indeed, the Appeal was upheld.  Did your car stall? Is that why you were in a yellow box?!

Euston Road/Duke’s Road

At the time that the motorist entered the box junction, the exit was clear, however, a car overtook him and cut in front of him stopping just the other side of the box junction.   The motorist therefore had to stop in the box junction.  The Appeal was allowed because “if it had not been for the vehicle cutting in from behind, he would not have been caught in the box-junction”.

The Appeal here was upheld because “the issue is whether there was any failure to comply with the sign”.  I find as a fact that when [the Appellant] entered Electric Lane with the car, the signage of the restriction was not clear.  The Appeal was upheld.  Time and time again we see Appeal cases where Councils are continually criticised for poor signage but fail to correct it and continue to enforce it which is actually Illegal.


CASE NO: 207 0012929 – City of London
Frogmorton Street

Here the Applicant stated that he stopped in the location to set down a passenger which is allowed.  Therefore the Appeal was upheld.   If picking up and setting down passengers is permitted and if this is all you were doing it is certainly worth pursuing an Appeal initially to the Council and eventually to the adjudicator.

Adjudicators decisions which relate to the contravention of failing to comply with a sign indicating a prohibited turn (Contravention Code 50)

In the case of Kennedy vs London Borough of Camden, Kennedy’s appeal was allowed because the parking adjudicator, stated “Having considered the evidence I cannot be satisfied that the manoeuvre can properly be described as a turn”.  The term “turn” suggests a change of direction directly from a prohibited place in one sweeping motion as opposed to a series of individual movements in which direction is changed.  Accordingly I am not satisfied that the contravention occurred and therefore allow the appeal.  So a turn is a turn in one complete motion!

CASE NO: 207: 020182A – Transport for London
Newcross Road/Watson Street

The Appellant stated that the no right-turn signs were obscured by other directional signage and a second sign was located too far down the road to allow the motorist sufficient time to avoid the prohibited manoeuvre.  The Appeal was allowed and the adjudicator also stated “furthermore as it is a no right-turn sign, it is a rather wrong footing to place the same on the left-hand side of the road for vehicles in the right-hand lane.  The more appropriate place for these signs appear to be at the traffic lights where they would clearly be more visible”.   The Appeal was allowed.   In order for any restriction to be enforceable the signage must be clear, not obscured or facing the wrong direction.

CASE NO: 207 0164246 – London Borough of Camden
Southampton Row, London WC1

This was an interesting case as a taxi driver did a ‘U’ turn after his fare asked to stop at a cashpoint.   He then did a second ‘U’ turn to return on his original journey.  However, due to his first ‘U’ turn he did not see the sign prohibiting the second ‘u’ turn which was further along the road and therefore the contravention did not occur.  You should note that the contravention is not that of making a ‘U’ turn it is that of failing to comply with a sign indicating a prohibited turn and therefore if there is no sign visible the contravention is not enforceable.

CASE NO: 207 0165952 – Transport for London
Newcross Road, SE14

The Appellant won this case on two bases.  First of all a single no right‑turn on the left-hand side of a carriageway was not considered adedquate signage especially if the sign could easily be obscured by a bus in the left-hand lane.  There should have been a sign on the right‑hand side of the road as well.  Secondly, the manoeuvre that was prohibited is a vehicle “turning into the adjoining road and although the Appellant’s vehicle clearly entered what might be termed the mouth of the adjoining road, it is by no means clear that it passed the building line of Newcross Road”.  The Appeal was allowed.  There should be clear signage and on a dual carriageway there should really be signage on both sides of the road due to the possibility of a sign at the kerbside being obscured by tall vehicles to motorists in the right-hand lane.  Also, the video needs to show your vehicle making a prohibited turn into the road and passing the building line of it.  In this case the clearly simply shows the motorist entering the mouth of the road but had not reached the building line when the video stopped.   We would always suggest that you ask to see a copy of the video or still photos showing the complete turn for which the authority seek to impose a penalty charge.

CASE NO: 207 0153002 – Transport for London
Grosvenor Place, SW1

As the Appeal was allowed because the contravention stated was that of “failed to comply with a sign indicating no left/right/’U’turn.   This is clearly void for uncertainty and accordingly this Appeal must be allowed”.   If you receive a Penalty Charge Notice, it must be for a specific offence.   Either the motorist was failing to comply with a sign indicating a no-right turn or a no-left turn or a no ‘U’ turn but surely not all three!

CASE NO: 207 0112741 – Transport for London
Battersea Park Road

This like the previous case concerned Transport for London issuing a Penalty Charge Notice for a no left/right/’U’ turn.  The adjudicator said “The penalty Charge notice is clearly void for duplicity or in the alternative uncertainty”.  The Appeal was allowed.  If you receive a Penalty Charge Notice, it must be for a specific offence.   Either the motorist was failing to comply with a sign indicating a no-right turn or a no-left turn or a no ‘U’ turn but surely not all three!

CASE NO: 207 0109011 – London Borough of Lambeth
Bedford Road

The Appeal was primarily allowed because the Council failed to provide photographs of the signage rather than sketch plans.  However, the adjudicator did state as follows:

“The recommendations of the Traffic Signs Manual 1986 [not law but highly persuasive] that at a signal control junction the sign should be mounted alongside the green aspect of both primary and the secondary signal heads.  It seems to me that where there are a pair of signal heads, one on each side of the road in each position this means the sign should appear on both but whether or not this is a correct interpretation of the words used, it seems to me that signing a no-right turn sign only on the left-hand side where it might be obscured by traffic is likely to be insufficient”.  The Appeal was allowed.  A prohibited no right-turn should ideally be signed on both sides of the road.

CASE NO: 207 0069868 – London Borough of Haringey
Green Lane

The Council provided still photographs and a video but no ‘no right-turn’ sign shown in either.  The Appeal was allowed.   As the offence is failing to comply with a sign indicating a prohibited turn, if the local authority are not able to prove the sign was in place, then the Appeal will be allowed as they are not entitled to enforce the restriction.

CASE NO 207: 0084985 – London Borough of Islington
Newington Green Road

The London Borough of Islington did not provide evidence of a no right-turn having been undertaken.  The driver maintained that he performed a ‘U’ turn rather than a ‘right turn’ and as the Council were not able to prove otherwise, the Appeal was allowed.  As a note, a no right-turn does not mean a no ‘U’ turn and vice versa.

CASE NO 207: 0049033 – London Borough of Ealing
Ealing – Uxbridge Road

This was an interesting case, the lady provided evidence that although the car in the photograph was similar to hers and had the registration number that in fact there were some details which were different from her own.  The adjudicator was persuaded that her car had been cloned and the Appeal was allowed.   If your vehicle is cloned, then you are not liable to pay a Penalty Charge Notice in the event of contraventions carried out by the cloned vehicle.

CASE NO: 207 0131611 – Transport For London
Grosvenor Place

The adjudicator said “the authority are under a duty to act fairly and to ensure that all restrictions are clearly marked.  The authority rely on inconsistent or incomplete evidence that does not satisfy me that the restriction was clearly marked.  In the circumstances, I cannot be satisfied that a contravention occurred”.  This was the Summary of Case where only one no right-turn sign was shown which was not deemed adequate.  Transport for London have maintained that there were two blue signs indicating that the cars may drive ahead only but there was no proof that they were actually in existence.  Many Appeals are won because of inadequate signage and some cases like this are won where the local authority are unable to even prove that the signs that they state are in situ are in fact in situ.

CASE NO: 207 010276A – London Borough of Lambeth
Westminster Bridge Road

“I have seen the CCTV video of this contravention.  The video shows the Appellant’s car turning right.  However, the video does not show any evidence of any sign indicating that a right-turn is prohibited and nor has the local authority provided any additional evidence of the sign”. The Appeal was allowed because there was no evidence that the no right‑turn sign was in situ.  Quite unbelievable that a local authority would try and enforce a no right-turn where in fact there is no ‘no right‑turn sign’.  The offence of that is that of failing to comply with a sign indicating a prohibited turn and not making a prohibited turn.

CASE NO: 206 0436849 – Camden County Council
Southampton Row

This was an application for a review of an adjudicator’s decision that found that adequate signage was in place.  However, when the reviewing adjudicator looked at the evidence, he allowed the Appeal because “I do not consider that the signage was adequate.  The placement of signs at junctions only suggest that they are limited to the junctions alone, how is a motorist to know that a no ‘U’ turn sign placed at a junction continues to provide a prohibition some considerable way further on”. He also mentioned “Direction 11 of the Regulations provides that repeater signs shall be placed at regular intervals and the 1986 Traffic Manual suggest intervals of 100m.  The Manual is not binding but advisory and designed to ensure clarity”.   This is typical of Camden Council in our opinion.  Quite disgraceful that they seek enforce ‘U’ turns without adequate signage in place.

CASE NO: 206 026977A – Wandsworth Council
Montserrat Road, London, SW15

The Appellant did not dispute that they failed to comply with a sign indicating a prohibited turn, however, the Penalty Charge Notice stated that the Appellant turned left in Montserrat Road rather than into Montserrat Road.   As the driver did not make an illegal turn while in Montserrat Road, the Appeal was allowed, even though the Council had pointed out that this was simply a typographical error.  Just as a motorist has to get it right 100% of the time, so do Council.

CASE NO: 207 0033237 – London Borough of Council
Newington Green Road

This Appeal was allowed because “there is no evidence in any form of a signage in place save of the words ‘Ahead Only’ on the road and the place where the turn took place.  The Appeal was allowed.  The correct signage was not in place – this contravention has not occurred and is not therefore enforceable.

CASE NO: 206 02491438 – Wandsworth Council
Putney High Street – 2 Montserrat Road

This case was most interesting as no turn was only enforceable at certain times.  The adjudicator said “There is no evidence here of any departure permission having been obtained from The Secretary of State, I am not satisfied therefore that this signing is lawful”.   The Appeal was allowed.   This is very interesting because there are many no turn signs which only apply at certain times of the day.  These signs are not lawful unless specific permission is given by The Secretary of State.  If you have received the Penalty Charge Notice at a location where the prohibition is part-time then you should ask the Council to provide the specific permission given by the Secretary of State for the variation.

Failing to comply with a no entry sign

CASE NO: 207 0038875 – London Borough of Southwark
Rye Lane

Here the Appellant won because only one “No Entry” sign was erected and in fact it was not clear as to which road it applied to.  “No Entry” signs and indeed other signs must be placed on each side of the road to which restrictions apply unless the road is less than 5m wide when a sign should be placed on one side.  (Most roads are much wider than 5m).  It is Direction 8(iii) of the 2002 Traffic Signs Regulations that stipulate this.  As there was one “No Entry” sign missing and the other was confusing as to which road it applied to, the Appeal was of course then allowed.   If the correct signage was not in place the contravention is not enforceable.

CASE NO: 207 0008863 – Borough of Lambeth
Larkhall Rise

As with the case before, the Local Authority had only erected a “No Entry” sign on one side of the road and therefore the offence was not enforceable and the Appeal was allowed.  If the correct signage was not in place the contravention is not enforceable.

CASE NO: 207 0107195 – Borough of Islington
Morris Place

This is an interesting case as the Appellant said he did not receive the initial Penalty Charge Notice because it may either have been intercepted or because there were so many flats in his block and the postman delivered to a central point.  The adjudicator said “that the crucial Penalty Charge Notice was not served effectively that is to say ‘actually delivered’”.   The Appeal was allowed.  The local authority has to prove that the Penalty Charge Notice is correctly served.  Simply posting it to a motorist is not proof that the motorist actually received it – an interesting point!

CASE NO: 2060397135 – London Borough of Camden
Kilburn Priory

“The video evidence showed the “No Entry” signs in place.  However, the road markings which appear to allow for normal two-way traffic and in particular the presence of a blue arrow indicating to traffic that it should pass to the left of the bollard seem to me to be, at best, confusing and worst, misleading.  In my view this is one of those cases where otherwise correct signage is undermined by the presence of other signs and I am therefore not satisfied that the indication given by the “No Entry” sign was sufficiently clear.  The Appeal was therefore allowed!  If there is a conflict between road markings and signs, then the Penalty Charge Notices are not enforceable as it is confusing for motorists.

CASE NO: 2-60166813 – Borough of Haringey
Tynemouth Road

The Appellant submitted evidence confirming that he was abroad at the date of the alleged contravention.  He had left his car keys at home but there was no-one in the house while he was away.  On his return, there was a problem with the window on his car and he produced a receipt for the work to this.  The adjudicator said “I found the Appellant a credible witness.  I accept his account that the car was not used by him or by anyone with his consent.  I do not find him liable for the Penalty Charge Notice”.   If the car was taken without your consent and you are therefore not the driver at the time of the alleged contravention, then it is not enforceable.

CASE NO: 206 0292035 – Transport for London
Battersea Bridge

The position on this is that the owner of the vehicle said that he did not receive the original Penalty Charge Notice.   Transport for London could only show that the PCN was printed.  But, in any event, the adjudicator accepted that the Appellant did not receive the Penalty Charge Notice and therefore the Appeal was allowed.   It is up to the local authority to ensure that a Penalty Charge Notice is received.

Failing to comply with a sign indicating restrictions on vehicles entering a pedestrian zone

CASE NO: 206 0511321 – Camden County Council
Laystall Street

The Appeal was allowed because “the video evidence supplied by the Council shows the Appellant’s vehicle leaving a junction near some railings and a ‘No Entry’ sign where this is in relation to the signage relied on and how it proves the contravention alleged is far from clear.  I cannot be satisfied that the contravention took place”.  The Appeal was allowed.  In this location, Camden continues to enforce the restrictions despite many adjudicators’ decisions saying they are not correctly signed.

 

CASE NO: 207 0063238 – Camden County Council
Laystall Street

The adjudicator said on the evidence before me, I cannot be satisfied that the Appellant’s car failed to comply with the sign.  The video does not show where the car would have passed the sign and in these circumstances I am not satisfied that a contravention occurred or that any restriction was adequately signed”.  The Appeal was allowed.  In this location, Camden continues to enforce the restrictions despite many adjudicators’ decisions saying they are not correctly signed.

CASE NO: 206 0100473 – Camden County Council
Laystall Street

The adjudicator said “I agree with the Appellant that the signage is sufficiently clear and this is confirmed by the large number of vehicles entering the pedestrian zone.  The road markings give no hint of the pedestrian area and the elevated sign is complex.   I suggest that the local authority give urgent consideration to the signage”.  The Appeal was allowed.   This Appeal was one year before other Appeals which were upheld on the same basis.  Camden Council has continued to illegally enforce restrictions that are not correctly signed.

CASE NO: 206 0096740 – Camden County Council
Laystall Street

This was an interesting one as the Council rejected the Applicant’s formal Appeal but on the Notice of Rejection it referred to the Applicant’s Right of Appeal “28 days from the date hereof” whereas the Act requires the reference to say “28 days from the date of service” – which must, ‘inevitably’ be after 28 days from the date of issue.   This is the second Appeal to be upheld on this basis, the first was by the National Parking Adjudication Service in the case of Lukha v Aylesbury Vale District Council.  The Appeal was allowed.  Not only must the wording on the Penalty Charge Notice comply but also the text on the Notice of Rejection must comply.

CASE NO: 206 0128905 – Camden County Council
Laystall Street

This was an interesting case where the Appellant’s cousin used his vehicle without his consent and incurred a Penalty Charge Notice.  The Appellant’s cousin has since returned to Turkey.  The Appeal was upheld by the adjudicator because “I am satisfied that he takes the benefit of the exemption which applies when the vehicle is controlled by another person who does not have the consent of the owner”.  If the vehicle is taken without your consent even by a relation then you are not liable for a Penalty Charge Notice.

CASE NO: 206 0493795 – Camden County Council
Laystall Street

This case concerned a courier who was allowed to delivery in Laystall Street because he was delivering, however, there was nowhere to stop so he drove through Laystall Street, parked elsewhere and returned to the address.  He provided documentary evidence that he had made a delivery in Laystall Street and therefore even though he drove through the street, his Appeal was upheld because he was actually carrying out a delivery.   The adjudicator said “I am therefore satisfied that he was at the material time unloading within the meaning of the exception”.  If you are allowed to enter a street for the purposes of loading and unloading then if you are unable to stop in the street but stop elsewhere and return to the street on foot, then you are still effectively loading and unloading in that street.

CASE NO: 206 0516507 –  Camden County Council
Laystall Street

In this case, the Appellant had a disabled badge which was not on display at the time.   However, she subsequently produced the badge and as there was an exemption for disabled badge-holders, the Appeal was upheld.

Quite why the Council did not cancel the Penalty Charge Notice before it got all the way to Appeal is incredible.
