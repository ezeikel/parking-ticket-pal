How to appeal a private parking ticket

How to appeal a private parking ticket (also know as a Parking Charge Notice)

 

We suggest that you read our sections parking charge notices and enforcement procedure before using this section.

 

Received a private parking notice?

 

Timescales:

 

Parking charge notice placed on vehicle or sent to vehicle keepers or vehicle hirers.

If the parking charge notice is sent by post the parking company must send this to you within 14 days of the alleged breach

You have 28 days from the date that you received it.(whether placed on your windscreen or received in the post) to pay the parking charge notice, challenge it or provide the details of who the driver was at the time.

If you pay within 14 days the parking company must offer you a discount of at least 40%.

If you do not pay or challenge it within 28 days the parking company may take further action.

If the parking charge is not paid within 28 days or if the breach of regulations is captured by CCTV a notice to keeper can be sent to the registered keeper of the vehicle.

 

NOTE: If you provide the parking company with details of the actual driver you can no longer be pursued by them and they must pursue the actual driver if they want settlement of their parking charge.

NOTE: If the parking enforcement company rejects your challenge then they must clearly set out in their rejection letter how you may appeal to the “independent” appeals service.

 

If you do not take any of the steps above the parking company may issue a notice to keeper and then take subsequent steps to recover the charge. If you make a challenge and they reject it you can appeal to the private parking appeals service.

For full details of the enforcement procedure please see our section enforcement procedure accessed from the left hand menu near the top of the page.

 

Appealing to the new “independent” parking appeals service.

P.O.P.L.A (PARKING ON PRIVATE LAND APPEALS) www.popla.co.uk

In 2021 41% of the 58,552 appeals to POPLA were successful! And remember that these are cases where the private parking company had already rejected an appeal to them and were confident of success !

You can only use the POPLA service if you have already appealed to the parking company who issued the ticket to you and they have rejected it. We strongly recommend that you use the service if you are not satisfied that the parking company have handled your initial appeal correctly. Do not be intimidated by any threatening letters which the parking company send. Once you have lost the discount offered you have nothing to lose by appealing.

You must appeal within 28 days of the rejection by the parking company.

The service is free to motorists so you probably have nothing to lose but will cost parking companies at least £27 each time a motorist appeals!

P.O.P.L.A does not allow personal hearings. We think it is the right of every citizen to be able to present their case personally. They accept postal and emailed appeals only.

Motorist’s appeals will not be handled by an adjudicator rather an “assessor” For issues which an “assessor” must consider and grounds for appeal please see our comprehensive section below.

If an assessor finds in your favour the parking company must cancel the parking charge however if your appeal is refused it is not binding on you. You do not have to pay it as you still have the option to go to court or to defend a case brought against you by the parking company in the county court. There have been a few court cases and these are summarised in our court cases section.

We are very concerned at the independence of POPLA having looked at the grounds for appeal on their website.

In considering appeals, POPLA is able to consider whether a landholder or parking company acting on his behalf has behaved reasonably. This includes whether any parking charges are based on a genuine pre-estimate of loss. POPLA can also consider whether the landholder has complied with the BPA’s Code of Practice, and inform the BPA of any suspected breaches so they may take appropriate action.

POPLA may also refer cases back to the landholder where it considers the landholder has failed to take reasonable account of evidence of reasonable mitigating circumstances which has been presented by the driver or registered keeper.

 

The Parking Charge Notice

If a contravention is detected remotely such as by cameras the parking charge notice must be sent to you within 14 days from the date of the alleged contravention.

If the driver/keeper wishes to appeal against the ticket he/she must make representations to the landholder within 28 days of the date of the ticket. Details of how to make representations must be included on the ticket.

As a legal minimum the Parking Charge Notice must say:

when and how the parking offence took place;

how much is due;

what any discount is for prompt payment of the charge;

how, when and to whom payment may be made;

the time and date when the notice was issued; and

what the arrangements are for the resolution of disputes or complaints – this includes any internal arrangements offered by the parking operator as well as any independent appeals process.

The parking charge notice must also include –

A unique reference number

The vehicle registration number

The make of the vehicle

Why the ticket was issued

A detailed location of the vehicle

An identifier number of the warden who issued the ticket

The operators company registration number

Details of payment methods and where and how challenges can be sent

A statement saying that the parking company will ask the DVLA for details of the registered keeper.

Any additional charges which may be levied.

Your letter to the enforcing company

Firstly, you should state the facts and your letter should be no longer than absolutely necessary. You should remember that the person who reads your letter may well have discretion to cancel your parking charge notice.  It may not be helpful therefore to vent your anger or make threats.  You should place yourself in the position of the person who will be reading your letter.  Photographs, diagrams, witness statements, delivery notes, pay and display tickets etc. can be extremely helpful.  Always send copies and keep the originals of any attachments.  You should also keep a copy of your letter.  Also remember to include the date and the number of the PCN in any correspondence.

If you receive a parking charge notice we would suggest that you take photographs of the site and particularly the signage.

 

Signage

We cannot emphasise enough how important clear and concise signage is as this forms the basis of the contract with the parking company. We have seen many signs and there have been many court cases where the signage was misleading, confusing, not prominent enough or simply where the letters were just too small. The position and number of signs is just as important. There should ideally be clear signs at all entrances to an area where parking charge notices are being issued and placed around the land. A motorist should be able to tell very quickly what restrictions are in place and how much the charge for non compliance is.

A parking contract as shown on the sign must set out the terms that apply, including the fees payable. It must also clearly set out the charges that apply if the terms of the contract are broken – for example, by parking for longer than the time paid for.

 

Signs

Operators are told that they “must give clear information to the public about what parking activities are allowed and what is unauthorised. The signs must not represent that the company is acting under statutory powers and no references can be made to a PCN unless the signs make clear that this in an abbreviation of “parking charge notice as PCN is also an abbreviation for a penalty charge notice as issued by local authorities under statutory powers. Words such as “fine” and “penalty” are also prohibited under the code. If the area is enforced using CCTV or ANPR then the signs must warn drivers of this and tell them what the images will be used for.

ENTRANCE SIGNS must be mounted at all entrances to a car park and tell drivers that the car park is managed and the type of restrictions in place. These signs must meet Department for Transport guidelines and be industry standard designs. They do not need to be over complicated and in fact you should be able to read all the information on them as you enter the car park.

The minimum height of the main letters must be 50mm if the speed of an approaching car is less than 10 MPH increasing to 120mm if the speed of cars approaching the signs is 40 MPH. The secondary information on the sign must be at least 50% of the height of the main lettering.

The signs do not have to be illuminated but they must be in a reflective material and they must be easy to read. They suggest the signage is white on a black background or viss versa. They rules say that blues and yellows should not really be used.

They must also display the BPA and AOS (approved operator scheme logos.

Specific parking terms signage – operators are told “you must place signs containing the specific parking terms throughout the site so that drivers are given the chance to read them at the time of parking or leaving their vehicle.” they are also told  that “signs must be conspicuous and ligible and written in intelligible language so they are easy to see,read and understand”

These signs must

be of a minimum size 450mm X 450mm

Specify the charge for unauthorised parking

Adequately bring the charges to the attention of the driver and

Follow any applicable government signage regulations.

Display the BPA and AOS logo.

You should appeal if you are not convinced that the signage or that there were not enough sighns. We urge you to read our court cases section.

 

Level of Parking Charge

A driver who is invited to park on private land and pay a fee does so under a contract with the land owner.

Any terms and conditions which either party wishes to enforce against the other are therefore subject to the laws of contract.

Charges for breaking a parking contract must be reasonable and a genuine pre-estimate of loss. This means that charges must compensate the land owner only for the loss which they are likely to suffer because the parking contract has been broken. For example to cover the unpaid charges and the administration costs associated with issuing the tickets to recover the charges. Charges may not be set at higher levels than necessary to recover business losses and the intention should not be to penalise the driver. So an example might be where parking costs £2 an hour. The vehicle is parked for 2.5 hours but only paid for 2 hours. A parking charge notice is issued for £100. Well the loss to the land owner is £1 plus the cost of issuing the parking charge notice. This might be no where near £100. In these circumstances an assessor could cancel or reduce the amount of the parking charge. This could also be a valid defence in the small claims court. In fact have a look at our court cases section as there are similar cases there where the motorist has won.

Where there is free parking which is time limited you may be able to argue that there is no loss unless the car park was full and therefore other customers could not park. This would be an interesting one.

Where a car parks on land where parking is not invited, the laws relating to trespass may apply. These are situations where previously clamping or towing was used but this has been outlawed

The landowner can make an “appropriate” charge and must be able to show he or she has suffered a loss and justify the damages they are seeking to recover for the trespass. The charge must be clearly displayed on signage.

They cannot impose penalties or fines on the public and cannot up the charge if you fail to pay within a certain time frame as this would clearly be a penalty. There is a lot of case law on what would amount to liquidated damages and what would amount to a penalty.

The British Parking Association has told its members not to charge over £100 and warned them that they may face a an investigation by the office of fair trading if they do.

 

A.N.P.R (Automatic Number Plate Recognition)

Private parking companies may use automatic number plate recognition systems. (ANPR)
However signs  at the car park must tell drivers that this technology is being used and what data captured by the system will be used for.

In order to use ANPR technology an operator must
Be registered with the information commissioner
Keep to the data protection act
Follow the DVLA requirements concerning the data
Follow the guidelines from the information commissioner’s office on the use of CCTV and ANPR technology.

For the code on CCTV use issued by the information commissioner’s office pleas click here.

If the parking company has not followed the guidance issued by the Information Commissioners Office then you may wish to consider making a complaint to them. They do act on complaints and we have complained to them on issues such as inadequate signage and had them rule in our favour. We also had a complaint upheld against Essex Police who refused to provide information to us on a speed camera on the M11 !

 

The rules which BPA (British Parking Association members must abide by.

The chances are that the company that has issued you with a parking charge notice is a member of the British Parking Association. (BPA) Only members of the BPA have to abide by their guidelines. You can use this section to check that the operator has complied and if not use it in your appeal.

Firstly parking companies are told that ” members of the public should be able to expect that you will keep to the law and act in a professional reasonable and diligent way”

Written authority – In order to be able to enforce the parking company must have the written permission of the landowner. If in doubt you could request this. At the very least you will then have the contact details of the Landowner. Usually the Landowner will set the parameters for example whether any parking is allowed and if so the charges or maximum stay period and also the grace period. If you want to contact the landowner directly you can find out their details by going to the land registry website. For a small fee you can obtain the details of the owner of the land.

Grace period – operators are told that they must allow a “reasonable” period for a motorist to enter the land, read the signs and leave if they choose not to stay. They are also told that they must allow a reasonable grace period after the expiry of paid for time or maximum time allowed has expired for a vehicle to exit the parking area. The grace period is not actually defined. We would say at least 5 minutes would seem reasonable.
